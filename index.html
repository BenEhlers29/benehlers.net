Preschoolers and Brand Identity

8 MAY 2017

I recently had the priviledge of speaking to a preschool class made up of 4 and 5 year-olds about the topic of brand identity and logos. Yes, this is a broad topic, but it was kept pretty basic. The discussion mostly centered around who makes those logos, and why are some of them so much easier to remember then others.

I was slightly concerned that I would not be able to grab their attention; however, I came prepared with a presentation full of products and branding that I hoped would command their attention. The presentation went better than I had expected. These kids were very aware of the branding they see in their world everyday. The designer in me was amazed at how in tune these youngsters are with a wide variety of brand identities. The free thinker in me was alarmed at the idea that we are producing a generation of mind numbed robots that merely respond to oft repeated patterns of colors, shapes, and text.

By the end of the presentation it became clear that kids just interact with advertising in a very "visual" way. They are very adept at matching an image with a brand message, especially if that brand is something they enjoy. I imagine this stems from the picture and word association games used in early learning tools. Some brands, like McDonald's for instance have hit the jackpot, while others, like Burger King have not sustained an iconic image and brand message that motivates children. The takeaway, (obviously) was that simplicity and consistency will always do bettter in the long run.

Once the presentation was over, I gave the the kids a sheet of paper full of shapes and had them use crayons to design their own logos. Once they finished, I grabbed a few of them and created vector mock-ups of how their simple ideas could make for impactful brand identities. I was really inspired by how easily the ideas were flowing onto the page, and how excited they were to see their creations transformed into real (fake) logos.
